Non-Traditional Machining Processes

Principles, process parameters, MRR, applications of EDM, ECM, AJM, USM, EBM, LBM, PAM, IBM. (Prof. Pramod — PRK)

🔍 Introduction to NTM

Traditional machining removes material using tools harder than the workpiece. Non-Traditional Machining (NTM) processes use alternative energy sources — electrical, chemical, thermal, acoustic — to remove material.

Why NTM?

  • New materials (ceramics, composites, superalloys) are impossible to machine traditionally.
  • Traditional methods are ineffective for very tight tolerances in micromachined components.
  • Need to avoid surface damage due to stresses in aerospace and electronics industries.
  • Complex 3D shapes and micro-features are achievable only with NTM.

⚡ Electric Discharge Machining (EDM)

Working Principle

EDM removes material by a series of discrete electrical discharges (sparks) between the tool (cathode) and workpiece (anode) separated by a dielectric fluid. Each spark produces a tiny crater by localized melting and vaporization.

Dielectric Fluid — Role & Properties

Dielectric fluid is critical in EDM. Functions:

  • Acts as insulator between tool and work until breakdown voltage is reached.
  • Ionizes to form a conductive channel for spark discharge.
  • Quenches the spark and restores insulation after discharge.
  • Flushes away debris (eroded particles) from the machining gap.
  • Cools the tool and workpiece.

Properties of a good dielectric: Low viscosity, high dielectric strength, chemically stable, non-toxic, readily available. Common dielectrics: kerosene, EDM oil, deionized water.

Spark Generator (Power Supply)

The spark generator provides controlled electrical pulses. Two types:

  • RC circuit (relaxation oscillator): Simple, capacitor charges from DC supply and discharges through gap. Produces uncontrolled pulse shape.
  • Transistorized (solid-state) generator: Provides controlled pulse duration (ton), pulse interval (toff), and peak current. Better control of MRR and surface finish.

Applications of EDM

  • Die sinking (making molds and dies)
  • Drilling small holes in hardened steel
  • Machining turbine blades and aerospace parts
  • Wire EDM for cutting complex profiles
  • Removing broken taps and drills from workpieces

🔋 Electro Chemical Machining (ECM)

Working Principle

ECM is the reverse of electroplating. The workpiece is the anode (+) and the tool is the cathode (−). Electrolyte flows through the gap. Material is removed from the workpiece by anodic dissolution (electrochemical reaction).

Process Parameters

  • Voltage: 5–30 V DC; higher voltage → higher MRR.
  • Current density: 20–200 A/cm².
  • Electrolyte: NaCl or NaNO₃ solution (0.1–3 M concentration).
  • Feed rate: Determines gap size and MRR.
  • Gap: 0.05–1 mm typically.

Advantages of ECM

  • No tool wear (electrochemical reaction, not mechanical contact)
  • No thermal damage to workpiece
  • Excellent surface finish (Ra 0.1–1.25 μm)
  • Suitable for very hard and brittle materials
  • Complex shapes possible

💨 Abrasive Jet Machining (AJM)

Working Principle

In AJM, material removal takes place due to impingement of fine abrasive particles (typically 0.025 mm diameter) accelerated by a high-pressure gas jet (0.2–1.0 N/mm²) through a nozzle onto the workpiece surface. Impact causes brittle fracture and dislodgement of material.

Process Parameters & Effect on MRR

ParameterEffect on MRR
Abrasive type (Al₂O₃ or SiC)Harder abrasive → higher MRR
Abrasive grain size (10–50 μm)Larger grain → higher MRR (up to limit)
Abrasive mass flow rateMRR ↑ as flow rate ↑; optimum mixing ratio exists
Gas pressure (0.2–1.0 N/mm²)Higher pressure → higher velocity → higher MRR
Stand-off distance (NTD)Increases first (particle acceleration), then decreases (atmospheric drag). Optimum ≈ 6–8 mm
Nozzle tip distanceAffects shape/size of machined cavity
Workpiece materialBrittle materials — higher MRR; ductile materials — lower MRR

Nozzle

  • Material: WC (life: 12–30 hrs) or Sapphire (life: ~300 hrs)
  • Orifice area: 0.05–0.2 mm² (circular or rectangular)
  • Abrasive reuse not recommended — decreased cutting capacity and nozzle clogging.

🔊 Ultrasonic Machining (USM)

Working Principle

USM involves a tool made of ductile and tough material vibrating at low amplitude (25–100 μm) and very high frequency (20–40 kHz). Abrasive slurry flows in the small gap between tool and workpiece. The tool hammers abrasive grains against the brittle workpiece, causing fracture and material removal.

Mechanism of Material Removal (M.C. Shaw's Model)

Material removal in USM occurs by:

  1. Direct hammering of abrasive particles on the work surface by the vibrating tool.
  2. Free impact of abrasive particles on the work surface.
  3. Cavitation erosion in the abrasive slurry.
  4. Chemical action of the fluid used.

M.C. Shaw's model assumptions:

  • Rate of material removal ∝ volume of work material per impact.
  • Rate ∝ number of particles per cycle.
  • Rate ∝ frequency of oscillation.
  • All impacts are identical; all grains are identical spheres.

Process Parameters & Effect on MRR

ParameterEffect on MRR
Frequency (20–40 kHz)MRR ↑ with frequency ↑
Amplitude (25–100 μm)MRR ↑ with amplitude ↑
Static feed forceMRR ↑ with force ↑ (to optimum, then ↓)
Abrasive grain sizeMRR ↑ with grain size ↑ (up to optimum)
Concentration of slurryOptimum concentration gives maximum MRR (~30–35% by volume)
Workpiece hardnessHarder work → lower MRR; brittle materials have higher MRR
Tool materialTough, ductile material (steel) wears much slower than workpiece

🔬 Electron Beam Machining (EBM)

Working Principle

A focused beam of high-velocity electrons (accelerated to 50–200 keV) is directed onto the workpiece in a vacuum. Electrons strike the work surface, their kinetic energy converts to heat, causing rapid melting and vaporization of the material.

Advantages of EBM

  • Can machine extremely small features (holes as small as 0.1 mm).
  • No contact between tool and workpiece — no mechanical stresses.
  • Can machine any material (metals, ceramics, semiconductors).
  • Very precise, programmable beam deflection.
  • High energy density — fast machining of micro-features.

Disadvantages of EBM

  • Must be performed in vacuum — high equipment cost.
  • Produces X-rays — radiation hazard, shielding required.
  • Limited to small workpieces that fit in the vacuum chamber.
  • Low MRR compared to conventional machining.
  • Heat affected zone (HAZ) is present.

Applications of EBM

  • Micro-drilling in turbine blades (cooling holes)
  • Semiconductor lithography
  • Precision drilling in watch parts and fuel injector nozzles
  • Cutting/welding of thin films

🔴 Laser Beam Machining (LBM)

Working Principle

A laser beam (highly coherent, monochromatic light) is focused onto a small spot on the workpiece. The high energy density causes rapid heating, melting, and vaporization of the material. No vacuum required (operates in air or assist gas).

Types of Lasers Used

  • CO₂ laser — most common for cutting metals/non-metals; wavelength 10.6 μm
  • Nd:YAG laser — pulsed; good for drilling and precision work; wavelength 1.06 μm
  • Excimer laser — UV wavelength; semiconductor and medical applications

Advantages of LBM

  • No vacuum required (unlike EBM) — simpler setup.
  • Can cut any material — metals, ceramics, plastics, composites.
  • Very high precision; narrow kerf width.
  • Non-contact process — no tool wear.
  • Can machine reflective and transparent materials with proper wavelength.

Disadvantages

  • High initial capital cost.
  • Heat affected zone (HAZ) present.
  • Low energy efficiency (typically 5–10%).
  • Reflective materials (copper, aluminum) are difficult to machine with CO₂ laser.

🌡️ Plasma Arc Machining (PAM)

Working Principle

In PAM, a high-velocity jet of ionized gas (plasma) at extremely high temperature (up to 28,000°C) is directed onto the workpiece, melting and blowing away the material. The plasma is formed by passing gas (argon, nitrogen, hydrogen, or mixtures) through a constricted arc.

Components

  • Power supply (DC)
  • Plasma torch (with constricting nozzle and tungsten electrode)
  • Gas supply system
  • Cooling water system (for torch)

Applications of PAM

  • Cutting hard-to-cut materials — stainless steel, titanium, superalloys
  • Profiling/contour cutting of thick metal plates
  • Thermal spray coating deposition

Limitations

  • Very high HAZ — metallurgical damage
  • Rough surface finish
  • Noise and UV radiation hazard
  • Limited to electrically conductive materials (in transferred arc mode)

⚛️ Ion Beam Machining (IBM)

Working Principle

IBM (also called Ion Milling or Sputtering) removes material by bombarding the workpiece with a focused beam of high-energy ions (usually argon ions, Ar⁺) in a vacuum. Ions transfer momentum to surface atoms, causing them to be ejected (sputtered) from the workpiece.

Characteristics

  • Operates in vacuum (10⁻⁴ to 10⁻⁶ torr)
  • Ion energy: 0.5–10 keV
  • Very low MRR — mainly used for nano-scale material removal
  • No heat affected zone (cold process at atomic scale)
  • Used in semiconductor industry for surface finishing and micro-fabrication

Electro Chemical Machining (ECM)

Principle: ECM is based on the principle of anodic dissolution — Faraday's laws of electrolysis. The workpiece acts as the anode (+) and the tool as the cathode (−). When DC current flows through the electrolyte between them, material is removed from the workpiece by electrolytic dissolution at atomic level. The tool shape is reproduced as a mirror image on the workpiece.

Process Parameters in ECM

ParameterRange / ValueEffect
Voltage (V)5–30 V DCHigher V → faster MRR but risk of sparking
Current density20–200 A/cm²Directly proportional to MRR (Faraday's law)
Electrolyte typeNaCl, NaNO₃, NaClO₃Determines conductivity and selectivity
Electrolyte flow rate10–60 L/minRemoves sludge and maintains temperature
Machining gap0.05–1 mmSmaller gap → better accuracy
Feed rate0.5–15 mm/minControlled by servo feed system
Electrolyte temperature25–35°CHigher temp → higher conductivity

Working of ECM

  1. Workpiece (anode) and shaped tool (cathode) are set up with a small gap (0.05–1 mm)
  2. Electrolyte (NaCl or NaNO₃ solution) is pumped through the gap at high velocity
  3. DC voltage (5–30 V) applied — current density reaches 20–200 A/cm²
  4. Metal ions are dissolved from the workpiece surface (anodic dissolution)
  5. The dissolved metal reacts with the electrolyte to form metal hydroxide sludge — carried away by electrolyte flow
  6. Tool is fed continuously towards the workpiece; the gap remains constant due to servo control
  7. The tool shape is replicated as a cavity in the workpiece
MRR = (AI)/(nFρ)
A = atomic weight, I = current (A), n = valency, F = Faraday's constant (96,500 C/mol), ρ = density

Advantages:

  • No tool wear (cathode does not dissolve)
  • No thermal stresses — process is electrochemical, not thermal
  • Complex shapes produced in one pass
  • Suitable for hard, tough alloys (Inconel, titanium, hardened steel)

Disadvantages:

  • High capital cost and maintenance
  • Not suitable for non-conducting materials
  • Disposal of electrolyte sludge is a concern

Applications:

  • Turbine blade profiling (aerospace)
  • Gun barrel rifling
  • Die and mould cavity sinking
  • Deburring of precision components

Ultrasonic Machining (USM) — Working Principle

USM removes material from a brittle workpiece by the abrasive action of fine abrasive particles suspended in a liquid slurry. The tool is vibrated at ultrasonic frequency (20–40 kHz) with a small amplitude (25–100 μm) perpendicular to the workpiece surface. The vibrating tool hammers the abrasive grains, which in turn impact the workpiece causing micro-fractures and material removal by brittle fracture.

M.C. Shaw's Model for MRR

M.C. Shaw developed the following theoretical model for material removal rate in USM:

MRR ∝ f · a · (d/D)^(1/2) · (H_w / H_t)^(3/4) · F

where:
f = vibration frequency
a = amplitude of vibration
d = mean abrasive grain diameter
D = diameter of tool
H_w = hardness of workpiece
H_t = hardness of tool
F = static feed force

Factors Influencing MRR in USM

FactorEffect on MRR
Frequency of vibrationMRR increases with frequency (up to 40 kHz optimal)
Amplitude of vibrationHigher amplitude → more impact energy → higher MRR
Static feed forceOptimal force exists; too low = insufficient hammering; too high = tool stalls
Abrasive grain sizeLarger grains → higher MRR but rougher surface finish
Abrasive materialHarder abrasive (B₄C > Al₂O₃ > SiC) → higher MRR
Slurry concentrationOptimal at 30–40% by weight; too thick = clogging, too thin = insufficient abrasion
Slurry viscosityLower viscosity allows faster grain circulation; higher MRR
Workpiece hardnessHarder workpiece → lower MRR; brittle materials machine faster
Tool materialDuctile soft tool (steel, copper) — tool deforms rather than fractures

Applications of USM:

  • Machining glass, ceramics, silicon, quartz
  • Drilling non-circular holes
  • Engraving on hard brittle materials
  • Machining semiconductors and piezoelectric crystals

Non-Traditional Machining (NTM) processes are those that do not use a hard cutting tool in direct contact with the workpiece. They use other forms of energy to remove material — electrical, chemical, thermal, or abrasive-jet.

Plasma Arc Machining (PAM)

Principle: A gas (argon, nitrogen, or mixture) is forced through a small constricted nozzle and an electric arc is struck through it. The arc ionizes the gas to form plasma — a state of matter at temperatures of 16,000–28,000°C. This extremely hot plasma jet is directed at the workpiece, melting and vaporizing the material.

Setup Components:

  • DC power supply (200–400 V, 50–300 A)
  • Plasma torch with tungsten electrode (cathode)
  • Constricting nozzle (copper, water-cooled)
  • Gas supply — argon, nitrogen, H₂, or mixtures
  • Workpiece as anode (in transferred arc mode)

Types:

  • Transferred arc: arc between electrode and workpiece — high energy, used for cutting
  • Non-transferred arc: arc within the torch — used for coating/spraying

Applications: Cutting stainless steel, aluminium, titanium, Inconel; aerospace component profiling; removing hard coatings.

Limitations: Large HAZ (heat affected zone); not suitable for precision parts; requires shielding due to UV radiation and fumes.

Electric Discharge Machining (EDM)

Principle: EDM removes metal by controlled electrical sparks between the tool electrode and the workpiece, both submerged in a dielectric fluid. Each spark produces a temperature of 8,000–12,000°C locally, melting and vaporizing a tiny crater of material. Thousands of sparks per second result in measurable material removal.

Process Steps:

  1. Tool (cathode) and workpiece (anode) are separated by a small gap (0.01–0.5 mm) in dielectric fluid
  2. Pulse generator applies high-frequency voltage pulses (DC, 50–300 V)
  3. Electric field ionizes the dielectric, forming a plasma channel
  4. A spark discharges — temperature reaches 8,000–12,000°C at the spark location
  5. Material melts and vaporizes; the molten material is flushed away by dielectric flow
  6. The dielectric de-ionizes between pulses (off-time), allowing the next spark to occur at the next closest point
  7. Tool is servo-fed to maintain constant gap as workpiece erodes

Process Parameters in EDM:

ParameterRangeEffect on MRR / Finish
Peak current (I)1–500 A↑ current → ↑ MRR, ↑ roughness
Pulse on-time (Ton)1–2000 μs↑ Ton → ↑ crater size → ↑ MRR, ↑ roughness
Pulse off-time (Toff)5–100 μsMust allow deionization; too short = arcing
Open circuit voltage50–300 V DCHigher voltage → wider gap → less precision
PolarityNormal / ReversedNormal: workpiece (+); Reversed: tool (+) for finishing
Electrode materialCopper, graphite, brassGraphite: high MRR; Copper: better surface finish
Dielectric fluidKerosene, EDM oil, deionized waterControls spark intensity, flushes debris

Role of Dielectric Fluid:

  • Acts as insulator between pulses (prevents continuous arcing)
  • Ionizes quickly during spark to form plasma channel
  • Cools the workpiece and tool after each spark
  • Flushes eroded particles out of the gap

Applications of EDM:

  • Die sinking: forging dies, injection moulds, stamping dies
  • Wire EDM: cutting complex 2D profiles (CNC wire cutting)
  • Micro-drilling: small holes (<0.1 mm) in turbine blades
  • Hard steel machining: after heat treatment — hardened tool steels
  • Aerospace: superalloy components (Inconel, titanium)

Abrasive Jet Machining (AJM)

Principle: AJM removes material by the erosive action of a high-velocity stream of abrasive particles (Al₂O₃ or SiC, 10–50 μm) carried by a high-pressure gas jet (air, N₂, or CO₂ at 0.2–1.0 N/mm²). The jet is directed at the workpiece through a nozzle. Upon impact, the abrasive grains cut micro-chips from the workpiece surface — a mechanism of brittle fracture and micro-cutting.

Factors Influencing MRR in AJM:

FactorEffect on MRR
Abrasive typeHarder abrasive (B₄C > Al₂O₃ > SiC) → higher MRR
Abrasive grain sizeLarger grains → higher MRR but rougher surface (optimal: 25 μm)
Abrasive flow rate (mass flow rate)↑ flow rate → ↑ MRR (up to saturation limit)
Gas pressure↑ pressure → ↑ velocity → ↑ MRR (optimal: 0.5–0.8 N/mm²)
Gas typeCO₂ or N₂ preferred; air causes moisture issues
Stand-off distance (SOD)Optimal SOD (0.5–5 mm) maximizes MRR; beyond optimal → dispersion reduces MRR
Nozzle materialTungsten carbide or sapphire — hardness reduces nozzle wear
Nozzle geometryCircular nozzle for drilling; rectangular for slotting
Angle of impingement90° for brittle materials gives maximum MRR; lower angles for ductile
Workpiece hardnessBrittle, hard ceramics machine well; ductile metals have lower MRR (material smears)

MRR Model (Finnie's equation for brittle materials):

MRR ∝ (v² · d³ · ρ_a) / H_w

where: v = abrasive velocity, d = grain diameter,
ρ_a = abrasive density, H_w = workpiece hardness

Applications of AJM:

  • Cutting, drilling, deburring of brittle materials (glass, ceramics)
  • Frosting/etching of glass and silicon wafers
  • Cleaning hard-to-reach surfaces
  • Trimming of thin resistor films in electronics
