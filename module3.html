🧱 Module 3

Powder Metallurgy

Definition, powder production, characteristics, mixing/blending, compacting, sintering, secondary operations, and applications. (Prof. Jinu — JNM)

🔍 Introduction

Powder Metallurgy (P/M) is a branch of metallurgy that deals with the production of metal and non-metal powders and the manufacture of components by using these powders. The powders are blended, pressed into desired shape, and heated to bond surfaces.

Why Powder Metallurgy is Important

  • P/M parts can be mass produced to net shape or near net shape.
  • Very little material waste — only ~3%.
  • Parts can be made with a specified level of porosity (filters, oil-impregnated bearings, gears).
  • Difficult-to-fabricate parts can be shaped (e.g., tungsten filaments for incandescent lamps).
  • Certain alloy combinations and cermets can only be made by P/M.
  • Production can be automated for economical manufacturing.

Limitations

  • High tooling and equipment costs.
  • Metallic powders are expensive.
  • Problems in storing and handling (degradation, fire hazard).
  • Limitations on part geometry — powders do not flow readily.
  • Variations in density may be a problem for complex geometries.

🔄 Basic Process Flow

Detailed Steps

  1. Manufacturing of metal powders
  2. Blending and mixing of powders
  3. Compacting — compressed into desired shape to produce green compact, using punch-and-die press
  4. Sintering — green compacts heated below melting point to bond particles into a hard, rigid mass
  5. Finishing operations (sizing, machining, impregnation, infiltration)

⚗️ Methods of Powder Production

1. Mechanical Pulverization

  • Crushing: Solid materials crushed by hammers, jaw crushers, gyratory crushers. Brittle materials → angular particles; ductile → flaky. Suitable for brittle materials.
  • Milling (Ball Mill): Most important and widely used method. Material tumbled in container with hard balls at controlled speed. Used for carbide-metal mixtures.
  • Eddy Mill, Rod Mill — other variants for fine grinding.

2. Atomization

Molten metal is broken into fine droplets which solidify into powder particles.

  • Water atomization: High-pressure water jet impinges on a stream of molten metal. Produces irregular particles.
  • Gas atomization: High-pressure gas (air, nitrogen, argon) breaks up molten metal stream. Produces spherical particles.
  • Centrifugal atomization: Molten metal fed onto a rotating disc; centrifugal force breaks it into droplets.
Most Important Method Atomization is the most widely used commercial method for producing metal powders. It produces nearly spherical particles with excellent flow characteristics.

3. Shotting

Molten metal poured through a sieve or orifice and cooled by dropping into water. Produces spherical particles of large size. Suitable for metals with low melting points (lead, tin, zinc).

4. Reduction

Pure metal obtained by reducing its oxide with a reducing gas at elevated temperature (below melting point) in a controlled furnace.

Fe₃O₄ + 4CO → 3Fe + 4CO₂ (Iron powder) Cu₂O + H₂ → 2Cu + H₂O (Copper powder) Also used for: Tungsten, Molybdenum, Ni, Cobalt

5. Electrolytic Deposition

Metal deposited on cathode from electrolyte solution. The deposited metal is scraped off, cleaned, and pulverized to get very pure powder. Used for copper, iron, silver powders.

🔀 Mixing and Blending

Blending — powders of the same chemistry but different particle sizes are intermingled to reduce porosity.

Mixing — powders of different chemistries are combined to create alloys or composite mixtures.

Purpose

  • Obtain uniform distribution of particle sizes.
  • Making alloyed powders by combining homogenous mixtures.
  • Combining metallic and non-metallic powders.
  • Ensuring filling up of interstices.
  • Binders added to enhance green strength during compaction.
  • Lubricants added to improve flow characteristics and reduce friction between particles and dies during compaction.

Equipment

Double cone mixer or Y-cone mixer are most common. Other geometries: cylindrical, rotating cube, twin shell.

Important Lubricants (zinc stearate, stearic acid) are critical — they reduce die wear, ejection forces, and interparticle friction. Binders (wax, polymer) improve green compact strength.

🗜️ Compacting (Briquetting)

The main purpose of compacting is converting loose powder into a green compact of accurate shape and size by application of pressure (80–1600 MPa).

The green compact must have adequate green strength for handling before sintering.

Effects of Compaction

  • Reduces voids between powder particles and increases density of compact.
  • Produces adhesion and cold welding of powders — gives sufficient green strength.
  • Plastically deforms the powder particles — allows recrystallization during subsequent sintering.

🔥 Sintering

Sintering is the heating of green compact to a temperature below the melting point of the principal powder to bond particles into a hard, rigid mass.

Purpose: Increase strength and hardness. Must be done in controlled, inert or reducing atmosphere (or vacuum) to prevent oxidation.

Mechanism of Bonding

  1. Melting of minor constituents in the powder particles.
  2. Diffusion between the powder particles.
  3. Mechanical bonding.

Sintering Sequence (Microscopic Scale)

  1. Particle bonding initiated at contact points.
  2. Contact points grow into "necks".
  3. Pores between particles are reduced in size.
  4. Grain boundaries develop between particles in place of necked regions.

Types of Sintering

  • Solid phase sintering: Heated above recrystallization temperature of low-melting metal (most common).
  • Liquid phase sintering: Above melting point of one alloy constituent, forming a liquid phase that aids bonding.

Sintering Stages

  • Drying stage — removal of lubricants and binders.
  • Actual sintering — residual stresses relaxed, particles deform plastically, voids close causing shrinkage.
  • Controlled cooling — to prevent cracking.

Critical Factors

Three Critical Factors Time, temperature, and furnace atmosphere control the sintering process.

🛠️ Pre-Sintering & Hot Pressing

Pre-Sintering

If a P/M part needs machining (drilling), it is difficult on fully sintered hard material. Pre-sintering heats the compact to a temperature well below the final sintering temperature so it gains enough strength for machining without being fully hardened. After machining, the part undergoes final sintering.

Hot Pressing

Process of compacting metal powders at high temperature — pressure and sintering temperature applied simultaneously. This eliminates porosity completely. Used when near-zero porosity is required.

  • Sufficient pressure applied at sintering temperature brings particles together.
  • Accelerates sintering — shorter cycle times.
  • Better mechanical properties than conventional compacting + sintering.

🔧 Secondary Operations

Secondary operations are intended to provide dimensional tolerances, improved physical properties, and better surface finish.

1. Sizing (Repressing)

Repressing the sintered component in a die to meet required dimensional accuracy, strength, and surface finish. The component is placed in a master die and pressure applied.

2. Impregnation

Oil or other fluid is permeated into the pores of sintered P/M part. Parts immersed in heated oil tank for a period of time. Vacuum application aids the process.

  • Products: oil-impregnated bearings, gears (self-lubricating).
  • Also: polymer resin impregnation for pressure-tight parts.

3. Infiltration

Pores of the P/M part are filled with a molten metal (filler metal must have lower melting point than P/M part). Filler metal drawn into pores by capillary action.

  • Result: nonporous structure with uniform density.
  • Improved toughness and strength.

Atomization — Metal Powder Production

Atomization is a process in which molten metal is broken into fine droplets by a high-energy jet of liquid or gas. The droplets solidify in flight to form powder particles. It is the most commercially important method for producing metal powders and can be applied to any metal that can be melted.

1. Water Atomization

  • High-pressure water jets (15–100 MPa) impinge on a stream of molten metal
  • Produces irregular-shaped particles (not spherical) due to rapid cooling
  • Most common method for commercial production of iron, copper, and steel powders
  • Particle size: 50–200 μm; powders may be oxidized — need reducing atmosphere sintering

2. Gas Atomization

  • High-pressure inert gas (argon, nitrogen) or air jets break the melt stream
  • Produces spherical particles due to surface tension during longer solidification time
  • Used for reactive metals (titanium, nickel superalloys) where oxidation must be avoided
  • Particle size: 10–150 μm; higher cost than water atomization
  • Very suitable for powder injection moulding (PIM) and additive manufacturing

3. Centrifugal Atomization

  • Molten metal is poured onto a rapidly rotating disc (1000–20,000 rpm)
  • Centrifugal force throws melt outward; droplets solidify in the surrounding atmosphere
  • Produces very uniform, spherical particles with narrow size distribution
  • Good for superalloys and specialty metals; expensive equipment

4. Vacuum Atomization

  • Metal supersaturated with dissolved gas is allowed to flow into vacuum
  • Dissolved gas expands suddenly, causing atomization of the melt
  • Produces clean, spherical particles free from inclusions

Metal Powder Compaction Methods

Compaction is the process of pressing metal powder into a desired shape (called a green compact). The green compact has a specific geometry but is fragile until sintered. Pressures of 80–1600 MPa are used.

1. Conventional Die Pressing (Cold Pressing)

  • Most widely used method; uses rigid die with upper and lower punches
  • Pressure applied simultaneously from both sides (double-action press) for uniform density
  • Pressure: 140–700 MPa for most metals
  • Produces net-shape parts with good dimensional accuracy

2. Isostatic Pressing

  • Cold Isostatic Pressing (CIP): powder enclosed in a flexible rubber mould, immersed in a liquid medium under high pressure (100–600 MPa). Uniform density in all directions. Used for large/complex shapes
  • Hot Isostatic Pressing (HIP): simultaneous application of heat and isostatic pressure; produces near-theoretical density parts. Used for critical aerospace components

3. Centrifugal Compaction

  • Powder placed in a mould rotating at high speed
  • Centrifugal force compacts the powder against mould walls
  • Suitable for producing cylindrical or tubular parts
  • Achieves non-uniform density (outer layers denser)

4. Slip Casting

  • Metal powder mixed with liquid to form a slurry (slip)
  • Slip poured into a porous mould (plaster of Paris)
  • Mould absorbs liquid; powder deposits on mould walls
  • Used for ceramics and special shapes; good for hollow parts

5. Powder Rolling (Roll Compaction)

  • Metal powder fed between two rotating rolls
  • Powder is compacted into a continuous strip/sheet (green strip)
  • Strip is then sintered in a furnace
  • Used for producing metal strips for electrical contacts

6. Extrusion

  • Powder mixed with binder to form a paste, then extruded through a die
  • Green rods, tubes, and sections produced
  • Hot extrusion: powder enclosed in a metal can and extruded at elevated temperature

Powder Metallurgy (P/M) is a manufacturing process in which metallic and/or non-metallic powders are blended, compacted into a desired shape, and then heated (sintered) to bond the particles together to form a solid part — all without reaching the melting point of the base metal.

Basic Steps:

  1. Powder Production: Powders produced by atomization, chemical reduction, electrolysis, machining, etc.
  2. Mixing/Blending: Powders of different sizes and compositions blended with lubricants and binders
  3. Compacting: Powder pressed in a die to form a green compact (80–1600 MPa); fragile shape held by mechanical interlocking
  4. Sintering: Green compact heated to 70–90% of melting point in a controlled atmosphere; diffusion bonding occurs, producing a strong solid part
  5. Finishing: Sizing (re-pressing), impregnation, heat treatment, surface finishing as required

Key Feature: P/M is a near-net-shape process — very little material waste; no machining chips generated.

Manufacture of Self-Lubricating Porous Bronze Bearing by P/M

Self-lubricating bearings are porous sintered bearings that hold oil within their pores. During operation, the oil seeps out by capillary action, providing continuous lubrication without external oil supply.

Step-by-Step Manufacturing Process:

1
Powder Preparation:
Electrolytic copper powder (88–90%) + tin powder (10–12%) + graphite (1–2%) are used.
Stearic acid or zinc stearate added as lubricant (0.5–1%) to reduce die wear.
2
Mixing/Blending:
All powders blended in a tumbler/double-cone mixer for 30–60 minutes.
Ensures uniform distribution of each constituent.
3
Compaction:
Powder filled into a cylindrical die and compacted under 20–50 kg/mm² (196–490 MPa).
Green compact has ~30–35% porosity — this porosity must be retained after sintering.
Die is coated with lubricant to ease ejection.
4
Pre-sintering (Optional):
Compact heated to ~400–500°C to burn off the lubricant (stearic acid) without sintering.
Prevents carbon contamination and cracking.
5
Sintering:
Compact sintered at ~800–850°C in a controlled atmosphere (hydrogen or cracked ammonia).
Tin-bronze liquid phase forms (tin melts at 232°C), filling some pores and bonding particles.
Final porosity: 20–30% (open, interconnected pores required).
Time: 15–60 minutes.
6
Sizing (Re-pressing):
Sintered bearing re-pressed in a sizing die to bring it to final dimensions.
Corrects dimensional distortion from sintering; achieves tight tolerances (±0.01 mm).
7
Oil Impregnation:
Bearing immersed in heated oil (SAE 10W or SAE 30) in an oil tank.
Vacuum applied to remove air from pores, then released — oil is drawn in by capillary action.
Oil content: 15–25% by volume of the bearing.
8
Final Inspection:
Dimensional check, oil content verification, bearing fit test.

Working Principle of Self-Lubrication:

  • At rest: oil held in pores by surface tension
  • During operation: shaft rotation generates heat → oil expands → seeps to shaft–bearing interface through interconnected pores
  • When shaft stops: bearing cools → oil re-absorbed by capillary action
  • Lifetime lubrication without external oil supply — ideal for inaccessible locations

Applications:

  • Small motors (ceiling fans, computer disc drives)
  • Home appliances (washing machines, blenders)
  • Automotive fuel pumps and starter motors
  • Business machines (printers, photocopiers)

1. Mixing and Blending

Mixing involves combining powders of different materials; blending combines powders of the same material but different particle sizes. Both are done in rotating equipment (tumbler, double-cone, V-blender).

Purposes:

  • Ensure uniform distribution of particle sizes (improve density)
  • Combine powders of different metals to form alloy compositions (e.g., Cu + Sn for bronze)
  • Add lubricants (stearic acid, zinc stearate) to reduce die friction
  • Add binders (wax, polymer) to improve green compact strength
  • Add graphite or non-metallic phases for special properties

Example: W + C (tungsten carbide powder) + Co (binder) — blended to make cemented carbide cutting tool inserts.

2. Compacting

Blended powder is filled into a die and pressed to form a green compact — a self-supporting shape held by mechanical interlocking of particles.

  • Pressure range: 80–1600 MPa depending on material and required density
  • Green density: 80–95% of theoretical density (porosity remains)
  • Lubricants in the powder or on die walls reduce friction and ejection force
  • Double-action pressing (from both ends) gives more uniform density distribution

Example: Bronze bearing compact: Cu–Sn powder pressed at 20–50 kg/mm²; green compact has 30–35% porosity.

3. Sintering

The green compact is heated in a furnace to a temperature below the melting point of the major constituent (typically 70–90% of melting point). Atomic diffusion causes metallurgical bonding between particles → strength and hardness increase dramatically.

Sintering stages:

  1. Initial stage: neck formation at particle contact points (bonding begins)
  2. Intermediate stage: pore rounding, densification, grain growth
  3. Final stage: pore isolation, further densification, grain coarsening

Sintering atmospheres: H₂ (reducing), cracked ammonia, N₂ + H₂ — prevent oxidation and decarburization

Sintering temperatures:

MaterialSintering Temp (°C)
Bronze (Cu-Sn)760–900
Iron/Steel1000–1300
Tungsten2000–2500
Aluminium alloys600–650

Properties of P/M Parts:

  • Porosity: 5–30% (controlled, open or closed)
  • Tensile strength: lower than wrought materials (due to residual porosity)
  • Good wear resistance (self-lubricating in bearings)
  • Good dimensional accuracy (near-net-shape)
  • Properties can be tailored by varying powder composition and processing

Advantages of P/M:

  • Near-net-shape — minimal machining, very low material waste
  • Can produce materials not achievable by casting (W-Cu, cermets)
  • Controlled porosity (self-lubricating bearings, filters)
  • High production rate for small parts (automotive)
  • Excellent dimensional tolerances (±0.01 mm)
  • Fine-grained, homogeneous microstructure

Disadvantages of P/M:

  • High tooling cost (hardened die sets expensive)
  • Limited part size and complexity (difficult to compact very large or very thin parts)
  • Porosity reduces mechanical strength compared to wrought parts
  • Powder production and storage costs are high
  • Not cost-effective for low-volume production
